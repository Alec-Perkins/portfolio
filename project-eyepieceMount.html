Selected design and CAD (SOLIDWORKS)

Most Practical Option

I chose the linear actuator design because it was the most practical given the money and tools available. The manual design would have required welding and tapping screw holes. I didn't have those tools at the time of this project. I also wanted practice building electronics, which the manual design didn't include. The tripod design had some of the same problems, and it was too expensive, so I went with the third approach.

Prototype

I connected the linear actuators and their drivers using the given adaptors. The drivers and actuators required 5 volts to be fully powered, so I used a portable battery connected through an Arduino I already had to provide a safe and constant voltage.


I didn't have time to 3D print the frame, so I used a Dremel tool to cut up some scrap metal for my frame.

I then zip tied a circular eyepiece clamp to the frame.


I fully attached both drivers to both actuators and powered them through a breadboard.

Then I used epoxy and more zip ties to connect one actuator to the other for two-axis motion and attached both to the frame.


I mounted the completed prototype on the test eyepiece. For testing, I used a cardboard box with protruding binocular eyepieces. This represented any telescope or microscope eyepiece, including binocular (double) eyepieces with limited clearance.

What I struggled with

Construction and Materials

The frame was designed to be 3D printed as seen in the CAD diagrams, but first it needed to be prototyped to make sure it would work. I had to improvise, searching my apartment for something I could manually repurpose as a prototype frame. My roommates then had to keep watch for Santa Barbara police, who might assume I was cutting the lock off someone's bike at 2AM.

Component Integration

The drivers and actuators were made by different companies with inconsistent color coding and terminology. It required a surprising amount of research and testing to integrate the components correctly. When I solved it, I took a picture of the correct configuration as part of the documentation. Then unfortunately the provided wires and terminal blocks connecting the drivers to their actuators would not stay connected. I had to clip off the terminals, solder each wire, and reinforce with electrical tape.

Actuator Strength

When testing the actuators, I quickly realized they did not have the torque needed to support the load of a phone. This might have been a design flaw caused by the cantilevered phone acting as a lever on the connection between the two actuators. (The problem with buying stronger actuators was the price.) To test, the binoculars were pointed at a blank door, and the image below was captured through my phone. It had to be supported by hand, but the actuators successfully positioned the camera lens into proper alignment with the eyepiece.
