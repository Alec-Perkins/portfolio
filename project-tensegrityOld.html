Tensegrity Chair - Jun. 2022

Woodworking • Strength Testing

Construction


I cut, sanded, and assembled the base with lap joints on each corner. This process was then repeated for the seat frame as they have the same dimensions.

Price and max load were two important considerations when buying the cables and turnbuckle in order to spend the least amount of money for a safe and reliable chair.


I measured out the angle necessary to have a comfortably positioned back support and attached the two backrest posts with wood glue and pin nails.

I then cut out the seat pieces and sanded, glued, and pin-nailed the seat support bars with as much accuracy as possible in order to prevent the pin nails from shearing off from the load.


Next, the triangle supports for both the base and seat were created with the exact angles necessary to reach each edge of the base and seat without being too tall to touch the ground or underside of the seat.

L brackets were placed on either side of both triangle support connections for stability and strength as twisting motion could again shear off pin nails and pry up wood glue.

I finally joined the two halves of the chair and threaded the tensioning cables through the base and seat and synched them tightly to maximize the stability of the seat.

Problems and Testing


What I struggled with

Tensioning Cables

Unfortunately, I made a mistake when purchasing the tensioning cables for this project. After attempting to synch down the cables and tighten the tension to balance the chair, I realized that the best construction should use very slightly elastic cables so I could set each cable in a state of slightly stretched elongation and they would be constantly forcing the seat and base together for far better stability.

Tools

In order to join the base and seat bordering cuts of wood together with lap joints, horizontal cuts needed to be made, halving each end edge of each piece along its side edge. I first attempted to use miter saw to cut down along the long edge of the wood, but this failed because the cut would have been a curved cut instead of a straight cut. Instead, I flipped the wood on its wider side and made many consecutive slices half way into the depth of the wood and chiseled the remaining pieces off for each edge. I then sanded the remaining surface for the strongest glue joining.

Installing triangle supports

The triangle supports had to be linked together before being attached to the base or underside of the seat or else I wouldn't be able to attach the base and seat together. Unfortunately, this made accurately screwing and gluing the triangle support to the base extremely difficult since the whole seat had to be already attached to the opposing triangle support.



Tensile Stress Testing

In order to test the strength of my joints, construction, and confirm my theory that the stainless steel turnbuckle was the weakest point of the build, I conducted strength tests. I first did some research about how tensile stress is measured and used the equation (stress = force/cross sectional area).

With this equation I predicted the turnbuckle would fail somewhere over 61.95 megapascals applied from the load on the seat using a radius of 2 millimeters of the screws in the turnbuckle to find my cross sectional area. This is because the turnbuckle I purchased was rated for 175 lb load. When I did further research, I found that the maximum tensile stress of stainless steel is about 310 MPa. I concluded that my predicted failure value was much lower because when the turnbuckle fails, it would not be due to the turnbuckle screws snapping at their cross sectional area, but instead due to the threading shearing off, making the maximum tensile stress much lower.

The data table above from my tests that followed these calculations more or less confirmed my theories, though the turnbuckle was much stronger than I expected!
