A little about me

My name is Alec Perkins. I’m a Mechanical Engineering major at UC Santa Barbara. I started coding in middle school, did front-end development, and later taught programming, but my personal projects usually combined art/design, software, and hardware. For that combination, Mechanical Engineering was ideal. As an undergraduate, I’m still building a broad base in fundamentals, but I am especially interested in Material Engineering for space exploration and biomedical applications to design life-changing medical devices.

Projects

High Alumina Ceramic Resin Printing - May 2024 to Present

In order to test the material strength of the ABAQUS lattice models described in the project below, I taught myself how to use a Tethon Bison 1000 DLP 3D Printer in the MMAX Lab. Using a ceramic and resin combination, the main problem to overcome is the difficulty of repeatability of this type of additive manufacturing. Small variations in environmental conditions cause large errors in printed results, skewing the desired material data. Even in perfect conditions, the models will sometimes come out distorted.

Spring-Mass System Simulation with MATLAB - Nov. 2023

I modeled an alternating sequence of springs and masses that is connected to walls at both ends. The system is initially at rest in equilibrium and is then set in motion by displacing the masses arbitrarily. I used eigenvalue and eigenvector analysis to derive equations of motion, MATLAB to simulate the system dynamics, and LaTeX for the PDF report.

Microscope / Telescope Eyepiece Mount - Jun. 2023

I wanted a convenient way to take high-quality photos through any microscope or telescope eyepiece using the phone cameras we all carry in our pockets. Every combination of phone and eyepiece is different, so I designed an adjustable mount. It holds any phone and provides fine adjustment of both x- and y-axis, using small stepper motors to bring the camera into perfect alignment with the eyepiece.

Tensegrity Chair - Jun. 2022

I built and tested a special kind of chair called a Tensegrity Chair. These chairs appear precariously balanced and on the verge of collapse but are completely stable. This is an illusion much loved by Engineering Statics professors because it’s like an interesting free-body diagram homework problem turned into furniture. I wanted to see for myself, so I built one. I then tested the load of the turnbuckle by incrementing the weight applied to the chair until the turnbuckle failed. These tests are documented in the report.
