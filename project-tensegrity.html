Tensegrity Chair - Jun. 2022

Woodworking • Strength Testing

Construction


I cut, sanded, and assembled the base with lap joints on each corner. I repeated this process for the seat frame, which had the same dimensions.

Maximum load and price were two important considerations when buying the cables and turnbuckle. The goal was to create a safe and reliable chair without spending any more than necessary.


I measured and cut it to the angle necessary for comfortable back support and attached the two backrest posts with wood glue and pin nails.

I then cut out the seat pieces and sanded, glued, and pin-nailed the seat support bars with as much accuracy as possible in order to prevent the pin nails from shearing off from the load.


Next, I built the triangle supports for both the base and seat. They had to be cut to the exact angles necessary to reach every edge while getting the seat height just right.

I attached L brackets to both sides of the triangle support connections for stability and strength. This would prevent twisting motion from shearing off pin nails or breaking the wood glue joints.

I finally joined the two halves of the chair and threaded the tensioning cables through the base and seat and cinched them tightly to maximize the stability of the seat.

Problems and Testing


What I struggled with

Tensioning Cables

Unfortunately, I made a mistake when purchasing the tensioning cables for this project. After cinching down the cables and tightening the tension to balance the chair, I realized that the best approach would be to use slightly elastic cables. Each cable would be stretched a bit, so they would hold the seat and base together tightly for better stability.

Tools

I did not have the tools needed to slice a board lengthwise to make interlocking lap joints. I tried several approaches before finally making small cuts, chiseling chips off the board, more cuts and more chiseling, and finally sanding it smooth to give it strong glue joints. Having the right tools for the job would have made it so much easier!

Installing triangle supports

The triangle supports had to be linked together before being attached to the base or underside of the seat. If not, I wouldn't be able to attach the base and seat together. Unfortunately, this made accurately screwing and gluing the triangle support to the base extremely difficult because the whole seat had to be already attached to the opposing triangle support.



Tensile Stress Testing

In order to test the strength of my joints and construction and to confirm my theory that the stainless steel turnbuckle was the weakest point of the build, I conducted strength tests. I first did some research into how tensile stress is measured and used the equation stress = force/cross_sectional_area.

The turnbuckle manufacturer claimed it was rated to hold a 175-pound load. I used the 2mm radius of the turnbuckle screws to determine the cross-sectional area. That cross-sectional area supporting a maximum of 175 pounds would mean the metal had a maximum tensile stress of about 60 megapascals (1 pascal = 1 newton per sq. meter.) But stainless steel is known to have a maximum tensile stress of at least 300 MPa, or at least five times as much. I assumed the difference was partly explained by a conservative load rating (a margin of safety), but even more by an expected failure mode where excess load would strip the screw threads before breaking the whole screw.

I tested it to failure (see table above). The results seem to confirm my hypotheses. It held more than 175 pounds (conservative rating) but not five times that much. The screw diameter was not the limiting factor. The threads stripped.
