Tensegrity Chair

Woodworking • Strength Testing

Construction


I cut, sanded, and assembled the base with lap joints on each corner. This process was then repeated for the seat frame as they have the same dimensions.

Price and max load were two important considerations when buying the cables and turnbuckle in order to spend the least amount of money for a safe and reliable chair.


I measured out the angle necessary to have a comfortably postitioned back support and attached the two backrest posts with wood glue and pin nails.

I then cut out the seat pieces and sanded, glued, and pin-nailed the seat support bars with as much accuracy as possible in order to prevent the pin nails from shearing off from the load.


Next, the triangle supports for both the base and seat were created with the exact angles necessary to reach each edge of the base and seat without being too tall to touch the ground or underside of the seat.

L brackets were placed on either side of both triangle support connections for stability and strength as twisting motion could again shear off pin nails and pry up wood glue.

I finally joined the two halves of the chair and threaded the tensioning cables through the base and seat and sinched them tightly to maximize the stablity of the seat.

Problems and Testing


What I struggled with

Tensioning Cables

• Unfortunately, I made a mistake when purchasing the tensioning cables for this project. After attempting to sinch down the cables and tighten the tension to balance the chair, I realized that the best construction should use very slightly elastic cables so I could set each cable in a state of slightly stretched elongation and they would be constantly forcing the seat and base together for far better stability.


Tools

• In order to join the base and seat boardering cuts of wood together with lap joints, horizontal cuts needed to be made, halving each end edge of each piece along its side edge. I first attempted to use miter saw to cut down along the long edge of the wood, but this failed becuase the cut would have been a curved cut instead of a straight cut. Instead, I flipped the wood on its wider side and made many consecutive slices half way into the depth of the wood and chisled the remaining pieces off for each edge. I then sanded the remaining surface for the strongest glue joining.


Installing triangle supports

• The triangle supports had to be linked together before being attached to the base or underside of the seat or else I wouldn't be able to attach the base and seat together. Unfortunately, this made accurately screwing and gluing the triangle support to the base extremely difficult since the whole seat had to be already attached to the opposing triangle support.
